Data & Tokenization — From the Messy Internet to Clean Integers

0. Where we are in the pipeline

In Chapter 1 we drew an 8-step loop. This chapter zooms into the first two:

  you are here

The headline you should leave with: the model never sees text. It sees a stream of integers. Everything in this part is about producing a clean, well-mixed stream of those integers — because that stream is the entire universe the model learns from.

1. What a training corpus actually looks like

Not "the internet" as a blob. A modern corpus is a deliberately mixed blend of sources, each chosen for what it teaches:

Source Roughly teaches Notes
Web crawl (e.g. Common Crawl) Breadth, everyday language, current topics The bulk by raw volume — and the dirtiest. Heavily filtered.
Books & long-form Sustained reasoning, narrative, depth High quality per token; licensing-sensitive.
Code (e.g. public repos) Logic, structure, and — surprisingly — general reasoning Improves non-code reasoning too.
Reference (encyclopedias, docs, Q&A) Facts, definitions, how-to Dense, high-trust.
Academic / technical Math, science, formal argument Hard, valuable tokens.
Synthetic (generated by other models) Targeted skills, rare formats, gap-filling Fast-growing; must be quality-controlled to avoid "model collapse."

Two ideas matter more than the list itself:

  • The mixture is a design choice. Engineers tune how much of each source to include (and how often to repeat it — "epochs"/"weights"). Up-weighting code or math measurably changes the final model's skills. The blend is a recipe, not an accident.
  • Scale is in tokens, not gigabytes or pages. Frontier models train on trillions of tokens. "Token" is the unit of everything from here on — which is exactly what Section 3 defines.

2. Curation: the data step is mostly deletion

Here is the principle from Chapter 1 — curation, not collection — made concrete. Raw web crawl is ~90%+ unusable. The pipeline that turns it into training data is a sequence of filters, each throwing data away:

  1. Extraction — pull readable text out of HTML; drop nav bars, ads, menus, boilerplate.
  2. Language & domain filtering — keep target languages; drop spam/SEO farms, adult/abuse content.
  3. Quality filtering — heuristics (too short, too many symbols, gibberish) plus a trained quality classifier that scores "does this read like the good stuff?"
  4. Deduplication — remove exact and near-duplicate documents. This is critical: duplicates make the model memorize instead of generalize, and waste compute. Often the single biggest quality lever.
  5. PII & safety scrubbing — strip personal data, secrets, and the worst content.
  6. Decontamination — remove text that overlaps with evaluation benchmarks, so test scores reflect real skill, not memorized answers. (Skipping this is how labs accidentally — or deliberately — inflate benchmark numbers; see Chapter 7.)
  Raw crawl  ─filter→ ─dedup→ ─quality→ ─safety→ ─decontaminate→  Clean corpus
  (petabytes)                                                     (trillions of *good* tokens)
     ░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░░  →  →  →  →  →  →                          ▓▓▓
     mostly thrown away                                           what's kept

Garbage in, garbage out is not a cliché here — it's the dominant lever on final model quality, often more than architecture tweaks. Curation is the craft of the data step.

3. Tokenization: why the model reads integers

A neural network does arithmetic on numbers. It cannot operate on the letter a or the word cat directly. So before training, all text is converted into a sequence of integer IDs called tokens. The component that does this — deterministically, both ways — is the tokenizer.

  "Tokenizing is fun!"
        │  tokenizer.encode()
        ▼
  ["Token", "izing", " is", " fun", "!"]      ← human-readable pieces
        │
        ▼
  [15496, 4954, 318, 1257, 0]                 ← the integers the model sees

The reverse (decode) turns the model's output integers back into text. The tokenizer is fixed before pretraining and shipped with the model — encode and decode must always agree.

Why not just use words, or just letters?

Tokenization is a compromise between two bad extremes:

Granularity Vocabulary size Sequence length Problem
Characters Tiny (~100) Very long Model wastes capacity relearning how letters form words; sequences explode.
Whole words Huge (millions) Short Can't handle new/rare/misspelled words ("out-of-vocabulary"); vocab unmanageable.
Sub-words ✅ ~30k–250k Moderate The sweet spot used by virtually all modern LLMs.

Sub-word tokenization keeps common words as single tokens (" the", " is") while breaking rare words into reusable pieces ("tokenization" → token + ization). Nothing is ever truly out-of-vocabulary, because the worst case falls back to bytes/characters.

4. How sub-word vocabularies are built: BPE

The most common method is Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE). It's a learned compression scheme: you run it once over a sample of the corpus to discover a good vocabulary, then reuse that vocabulary forever.

The algorithm is simple enough to follow by hand:

  1. Start with the smallest units (bytes/characters) as your vocabulary.
  2. Count every adjacent pair across the data.
  3. Merge the single most frequent pair into a new token; add it to the vocabulary.
  4. Repeat until you hit a target vocabulary size (e.g. 50,000 merges).

A tiny worked example

Suppose our entire corpus is just the word low repeated, plus lower, lowest, newest, widest. Start as characters:

 l o w / l o w e r / l o w e s t / n e w e s t / w i d e s t
  • The pair e s is very frequent (lowest, newest, widest) → merge → es.
  • Now es t is frequent → merge → est.
  • l o appears in low, lower, lowest → merge → lo, then lo w → low.

After a few merges the vocabulary contains useful chunks: est, low, er, … So a new word like slowest tokenizes neatly as s + low + est — pieces it has seen, even though the whole word is new. That generalization is the entire point.

Modern tokenizers operate on raw bytes (so any character in any language, plus emoji, always encodes), and bake whitespace into tokens — which is why you'll see leading-space tokens like " fun" rather than "fun".

5. Special tokens and structure

Beyond ordinary text, tokenizers reserve a handful of special tokens that carry structural meaning rather than content:

  • End-of-text / document boundary — tells the model where one document stops.
  • Chat role markers — e.g. tokens that delimit system, user, and assistant turns. These are how a chat model knows "this part is the user talking, this part is me." (Central to Chapter 5 — alignment.)
  • Padding / unknown / tool-call markers, depending on the system.

This is a quiet but important point: the conversation structure you experience as "roles" is, underneath, just more tokens in the same stream.

6. Why this matters in practice

Tokens aren't an internal detail you can ignore — they're the unit of cost, speed, and limits:

  • Context window (e.g. 200K tokens) is measured in tokens, not words. Rough English rule of thumb: 1 token ≈ 4 characters ≈ ¾ of a word, so ~750 words ≈ 1,000 tokens. (Varies by language and content.)
  • Price and latency are per-token. Longer prompts and outputs cost proportionally more.
  • Non-English text and code cost more tokens for the same meaning, because the tokenizer was optimized mostly on English — a real fairness/efficiency issue.
  • Tokenization explains weird failures. The classic "how many r's in strawberry?" stumble happens partly because the model sees straw + berry as a couple of tokens, not nine letters — it never had the letters as separate units. Likewise, arithmetic on long numbers is hard partly because of how digits get chunked.

Mental model: the tokenizer is the model's senses. It can only perceive the world in the chunks the tokenizer hands it. Many "why did the AI do that?" mysteries dissolve once you remember it's reading tokens, not characters or words.

7. Recap

  • The model never sees text — only a stream of integer tokens. The data pipeline exists to make that stream clean and well-mixed.
  • A corpus is a deliberate blend of sources (web, books, code, reference, synthetic), measured in trillions of tokens. The mixture is a tuned recipe.
  • Curation is mostly deletion: extract → filter → deduplicate → scrub → decontaminate. This is the dominant quality lever.
  • Sub-word tokenization (usually BPE) is the compromise between characters and words: common words stay whole, rare words split into reusable pieces, nothing is out-of-vocabulary.
  • Tokens are the unit of context size, cost, and speed, and they explain a whole class of model quirks.
