# Building a Modern AI System — The Top-Level Map

> *Foundations of Modern AI* — Chapter 1
> Scope: the end-to-end lifecycle of a modern AI system, at the altitude of "major steps."
> Focus model: Large Language Models (LLMs) like the GPT / Claude / Gemini families, because they are the dominant paradigm today. Most steps generalize to other AI systems.

---

## 1. The big picture

A modern AI system is built along a spine of four ideas: **data → train → refine → use**. That spine is correct, but it hides the joints that make the whole thing work — how raw data becomes model-readable, what the model actually *is*, how you know it works, and how it survives contact with real users.

Filling those joints in gives **eight major steps**:

| # | Step | One-sentence purpose |
|---|------|----------------------|
| 1 | **Data collection & curation** | Gather, filter, deduplicate, and clean a huge, high-quality corpus. |
| 2 | **Preprocessing & tokenization** | Turn text into the integer tokens the model actually consumes. |
| 3 | **Model architecture & design** | Choose the network (almost always a Transformer) and its size. |
| 4 | **Pretraining** | Self-supervised next-token prediction over the whole corpus — the expensive part. |
| 5 | **Post-training & alignment** | SFT + RLHF/preference tuning to make it helpful, honest, and safe. |
| 6 | **Evaluation & red-teaming** | Measure capability and safety before and after shipping. |
| 7 | **Deployment & inference** | Serve the model efficiently; add prompting, retrieval, tools, agents. |
| 8 | **Monitoring & iteration** | Watch real usage, catch failures, feed learnings back to step 1. |

It is a **loop, not a line**: step 8 feeds step 1 for the next model generation.

## 2. Each step, briefly

### Step 1 — Data collection & curation
Sources: web crawls (e.g. Common Crawl), books, code, reference works, licensed datasets, and increasingly *synthetic* data generated by other models. **The work is removal, not collection:** strip boilerplate and spam, deduplicate (near-duplicates hurt), filter for quality and safety, remove personal data, and respect licensing. The slogan that holds up: *garbage in, garbage out.* A smaller clean corpus beats a larger dirty one.

> You don't want "all text on the internet." Most of the internet is low-value for training, and some of it is actively harmful. **Curation is the craft** of this step.

### Step 2 — Preprocessing & tokenization
Models don't read characters or words — they read **tokens**, integer IDs for sub-word chunks (e.g. `"tokenization"` → `token` + `ization`). A *tokenizer* (commonly Byte-Pair Encoding) is learned from the data. Token count is the unit of nearly everything downstream: context-window size, cost, and speed are all measured in tokens. This step is the bridge between raw text and the math.

### Step 3 — Model architecture & design
Today this almost always means a **Transformer** (the 2017 "Attention Is All You Need" design), whose *attention* mechanism lets every token weigh every other token. Key choices: number of parameters (billions to trillions), number of layers, context-window length, and variants like **Mixture-of-Experts** (only part of the network runs per token, for efficiency). Architecture is *chosen by humans*; the parameters inside it are *learned by training*.

### Step 4 — Pretraining
The model is shown text and repeatedly asked to **predict the next token**; each wrong guess nudges its billions of parameters via gradient descent. This is **self-supervised** — the "labels" are just the next words already in the text, so no humans annotate anything. It is also the dominant *cost*: months of thousands of GPUs/TPUs. The output is a **base model** that has absorbed grammar, facts, reasoning patterns, and code — but doesn't yet reliably *follow instructions* or behave as an assistant.

### Step 5 — Post-training & alignment
This turns a base model into a usable assistant, and it's where humans enter the loop:
- **Supervised fine-tuning (SFT):** train on curated examples of good instruction-following (often human-written demonstrations).
- **Preference optimization / RLHF:** humans (or AI judges — "RLAIF") rank competing answers; the model is tuned to prefer the better ones. Methods include RLHF and DPO.
- **Safety alignment:** teach refusal of harmful requests, honesty about uncertainty, and tone.

Comparatively cheap vs. pretraining, but it has an outsized effect on how the model *feels* to use.

### Step 6 — Evaluation & red-teaming
Before and after shipping, measure:
- **Capability:** benchmarks (math, coding, reasoning, knowledge) and increasingly human/LLM preference judging.
- **Safety:** **red-teaming** — deliberately trying to make the model misbehave — plus bias and robustness testing.

The hard, unsolved part: benchmarks get "gamed" or leak into training data, so evaluation is a moving target. This step is easy to overlook, yet a lot of real engineering effort lives here.

### Step 7 — Deployment & inference
**Inference** is the act of running the trained model to generate output, one token at a time (each new token is appended and fed back in: *autoregressive* generation). Around the raw model sits a system:
- **Serving & optimization:** quantization, batching, caching to make it fast and cheap.
- **Prompting & context:** system prompts, the context window, few-shot examples.
- **Retrieval (RAG):** fetch relevant documents at query time so the model can cite current/private data it wasn't trained on.
- **Tools & agents:** let the model call functions, search, run code, and take multi-step actions.

This is where most *application* developers actually work — the model is a component, not the whole product.

### Step 8 — Monitoring & iteration
Once real users arrive: log failures, watch for drift and abuse, gather feedback, patch with guardrails, and feed lessons into the **next** data/training cycle. This closes the loop — modern AI is built in *generations*, not once.

## 3. Mental model to carry forward

Three reframes that will pay off as we go deeper:

1. **Curation, not collection.** The data step is about what you *throw away*.
2. **Two very different training phases.** Pretraining (huge, automatic, builds raw capability) vs. post-training (smaller, human-guided, builds behavior). Treating them as one undifferentiated "training" is the most common beginner mistake — keep them firmly separate in your mind.
3. **The model is a component.** The thing users touch is model + prompting + retrieval + tools + guardrails. "Using an AI system" is rarely just calling a raw model.
